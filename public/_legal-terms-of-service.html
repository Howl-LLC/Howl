7. End-to-End Encryption

Howl provides end-to-end encryption ("E2E Encryption"), using the Messaging Layer Security protocol (MLS, RFC 9420), for all direct messages, group direct messages, and direct-message voice and video calls. Messages, file attachments, and call media are encrypted on your device before transmission and can only be decrypted by the intended participants' devices. In Maximum Privacy mode (the default), Howl's servers process only opaque ciphertext for those conversations and cannot read, search, scan, index, moderate, or disclose their plaintext content. If you opt into Secure & Easy mode, Howl holds a recoverable backup of your keys, as described in Section 7.2.

Howl offers two modes for managing your encryption keys:

7.1 Maximum Privacy mode (default)

Your encryption keys are derived from a passphrase you set and are stored only on your devices. Howl holds no copy of your passphrase or keys. If you lose both your passphrase and your recovery key, your E2E-encrypted data is permanently unrecoverable. In Maximum Privacy mode you are solely responsible for safely storing your passphrase and recovery key. We cannot reset your passphrase or recover your data for you.

7.2 Secure & Easy mode (opt-in)

When you enable Secure & Easy mode, Howl stores a password-protected backup of your encryption keys ("escrow blob"), encrypted with a key derived from your account password. This provides a recovery path if you forget your passphrase: after resetting your account password, you can complete the server-recovery flow from Settings → Encryption on your next login to regain access to your E2E-encrypted data (the recovery is not performed automatically as part of the password reset itself). In this mode, Howl LLC and authorized personnel could, in principle, derive your encryption keys if compelled by valid legal process combined with access to your account password (a capability we do not use absent such process). Secure & Easy mode trades a small amount of privacy for recoverability. You can switch between Maximum Privacy and Secure & Easy at any time in Settings → Encryption.

7.3 Metadata and Non-E2E Surfaces

E2E Encryption protects message and call content only. Metadata necessary to deliver the Service, including participant identifiers, timestamps, message sizes, channel membership, and related technical signals, remains visible to our servers for all conversations. Server text channels and other non-E2E surfaces are not end-to-end encrypted and are subject to our moderation, enforcement, and legal-process obligations as described in our Privacy Policy and Law Enforcement Guidelines.

7.4 Device and Credential Security

E2E Encryption does not protect against compromise of your device, your account credentials, or an unauthorized party who gains access to your unlocked session. You are responsible for securing your devices, credentials, and the physical environment from which you access the Service.

8. Voice and Video Communications

The Service includes voice channels, video calls, direct-message voice and video calls, screen sharing, and other real-time communications, delivered using a selective-forwarding-unit (SFU) architecture based on LiveKit.

  • Server voice channels use SFrame end-to-end encryption between participants, carried over a DTLS-SRTP transport. The per-channel key is distributed among the participants' devices, and our media servers forward the encrypted frames without access to the media content. Howl does not record or store server voice-channel media.
  • Direct-message voice and video calls use SFrame end-to-end encryption between participants. The per-call encryption key is derived from the conversation's end-to-end-encrypted (MLS) group and is available only to the call participants' devices. Our media servers forward the encrypted frames without access to the media content.

You must obtain consent from all participants before recording any voice or video communication conducted through the Service. Unauthorized recording may violate applicable wiretapping or privacy laws in your jurisdiction.

We do not actively monitor the content of voice or video communications. Reports of abuse in voice and video may rely on participant-submitted evidence.
